Withdrawal, Blood-borne Infections and Contaminates


Aside from the obvious and immediate risk of an overdose there are other long term harms accosted with drug use


Withdrawal

Most illicit drugs including alcohol are extremely addictive to the point where the substance takes over the addict's life, pushing loved ones away. If an addicted person trys to stop using an addictive drug suddenly or to quickly then can expiearance severe symptoms including:


  • craving (a strong desire to use the drug)
  • confusion.
  • feeling angry and upset
  • severe stomach upset, vomiting
  • depression
  • seizures
  • Death

Fun? Fact: Alcohol withdrawal can be fatal



Blood-borne Infections

Intravenous drug use significantly increases the risk of contracting blood-borne infections such as HIV, hepatitis B, and hepatitis C through the sharing of contaminated needles and other drug paraphernalia. According to CDC data, approximately 1 in 10 new HIV diagnoses are attributed to injection drug use.



Contaminates

The contamination of illicit drugs, particularly with potent synthetic opioids like fentanyl, has become a major public health crisis, leading to a surge in overdose deaths among users who may be unaware of the presence of these dangerous adulterants. A study by the Drug Enforcement Administration found that 42% of pills tested for fentanyl contained a potentially lethal dose, underscoring the extreme risks associated with consuming unregulated street drugs.

Harm Reduction Strategies for Substance Use



Harm reduction strategies can significantly decrease the risks associated with substance use. Here are some approaches to keep safe:



  • Never use drugs alone. Have a trusted friend present
  • Start with a small dose, especially when trying a new substance.
  • Keep naloxone (Narcan) on hand and know how to use it in case of opioid overdose.
  • Use fentanyl test strips to check for contamination in drugs.
  • If injecting, always use clean, sterile equipment and never share needles
  • Plan safe transportation in advance to avoid driving under the influence.
  • Be aware of drug interactions, including with prescription medications and alcohol.
  • Seek medical help immediately if experiencing adverse effects or overdose symptoms.
  • Access addiction support services or counseling if struggling with substance use.

Educating oneself and others about these strategies can create a safer environment for those who use substances, potentially saving lives and reducing long-term health consequences.



















The Changing Face of Substance Abuse

Harm Reduction is Even More Important Because Substance Abuse is Becoming More Dangerous





The landscape of drug use has undergone significant shifts in recent years, with notable changes in both the types of substances consumed and the demographics of users. Opioid abuse, once primarily associated with prescription painkillers, has seen a dramatic rise in synthetic opioid use, with fentanyl-related overdose deaths increasing by 1,125% between 2011 and 2021. Given that the number of drug users has remained relatively constant this highlights just how much more dangerous it is to use drugs (You would have a 1,125% greater chance of overdosing then ~10 years ago)
